Research Journal · Project 02

Pieces of Power

Eight readings that shaped a pavilion where the chess set is a monument to hierarchy.

Max McDonough · April 2026

Before any Blender file was opened, I spent a week reading about where the chess hierarchy came from, why it still feels legible a millennium after its invention, and which contemporary systems of power it secretly maps onto. The eight insights below are the ones that changed the direction of the pavilion. Some are historical, some architectural, some about how a fictional world (the wizarding one) can render real hierarchies legible in a way an org chart never can. Together they pushed the project away from a literal corporate analogue and toward a layered reading where the piece is not a single role — it is a stack of overlapping ones.

01
Origin

Chaturanga encoded a real army, not an abstract one

The 6th-century Indian precursor to chess — chaturanga, literally “four limbs” — arranged its pieces around the four divisions of a working Gupta-era army: infantry, cavalry, elephantry, and chariotry. The board was a military diagram, not a metaphor for one. What we read today as “pawn” and “knight” were literal reports of who showed up to fight. This means the chessboard began as an inventory of real power — a reality the project restores by re-anchoring each piece to named roles in a real (if fictional) institution.

Source: encyclopedia entries on Chaturanga; British Museum commentary on chess' transmission through Persia.

02
Feudalism

Europe re-cast chess as a court, and the hierarchy froze

When the game travelled west through Persia and Islamic Spain into medieval Europe, the elephant became the bishop, the chariot became the rook (a castle), the minister became the queen — and over several centuries the queen gained the most mobility on the board at exactly the moment historical queens like Isabella I of Castile were consolidating state power. The piece names are artefacts of which hierarchy the adopting culture wanted to enshrine. Any re-mapping I do is simply the next layer in a thousand-year tradition of pieces shape-shifting to mirror their host society.

Source: Marilyn Yalom, Birth of the Chess Queen (2004); British Library's online chess history collection.

03
Doctrine

Power runs on scheduled sacrifice

Clausewitz' On War treats the intentional loss of lower-ranked units as a first-class tool of strategy, not a failure mode. The Philosopher's Stone wizard's-chess scene is a compact cinematic demonstration of the same doctrine: the knight voluntarily takes the blow so the bishop can deliver checkmate. In corporate analogues this shows up as layoffs announced as “focusing”; in military hierarchies as “acceptable losses.” The pavilion treats the Knight as the pivotal piece precisely because its signature move — the jump across ranks — only activates when something below it is spent.

Source: Clausewitz, On War, Book III; J. Silman's published account of designing the film's chess choreography.

04
Architecture

Verticality is the oldest signal of rank

Hofstede's cross-cultural studies describe a variable called power distance — how comfortable a society is with unequal authority. High-power-distance cultures tend to produce buildings with dramatic vertical stratification: soaring cathedrals, elevated thrones, colonnaded assembly halls. The Hogwarts Great Hall does this explicitly: high table on a raised dais, house tables on the floor, floating candles drawing the eye upward. The pavilion borrows the gesture — the chamber is deliberately tall, the four pieces sit on pedestals, and the ruby altar is elevated — so rank is readable before any caption fires.

Source: Geert Hofstede, Culture's Consequences (2001); cross-comparison with Gothic cathedral plans.

05
Parallel systems

The wizarding world stacks four hierarchies into one

Rowling's world layers multiple power pyramids on the same cast of characters. A student like Harry is simultaneously: low-ranked in the school (first year), high-ranked in the prophecy (the chosen one), rank-agnostic in the Order (a field operative), and a piece in a longer generational war. That stacking is what makes the wizarding world useful for a chess brief — the same figure can be a pawn and a king depending on which hierarchy you're reading at the moment. The pavilion leans into this by refusing to collapse each piece to a single label.

Source: comparative readings of Rowling's novels; essays on power in the Potterverse (Jones & Reschke, 2009).

06
Organisation

Hierarchies exist whether we acknowledge them

Jo Freeman's 1970 essay The Tyranny of Structurelessness argues that “flat” organisations still have hierarchies — they are just unacknowledged and therefore unaccountable. The intellectual pay-off is that chess is actually the honest organisation chart: every piece has an explicit rank, a published move set, and a visible consequence for being captured. Framing chess as honest hierarchy rather than oppressive hierarchy reframes the pavilion from critique to monument — the pieces are held up as legible power rather than hidden away.

Source: Jo Freeman, The Tyranny of Structurelessness (1970); cross-reference with contemporary flat-org critiques (Valve, Zappos case studies).

07
Material

Materials are status signals before anyone reads the label

Veblen's conspicuous consumption showed that material choice communicates rank independent of any caption. In Hogwarts that reads as: the headmaster's office has gilded instruments, the prefects' bathroom has marble, the common rooms have wood and tapestry, and student dormitories have stone. The chess set adopts the same grammar: sculpted sandstone bodies for all four pieces with darkened iron crowns that grow in weight and contrast as rank increases. Material does the hierarchy work before silhouette does.

Source: Thorstein Veblen, The Theory of the Leisure Class (1899); architectural-materials analyses of Gothic institutional buildings.

08
Scale

Scale decides who is the subject of the scene

In the film's chess sequence the pieces are monstrous relative to the three children — a deliberate inversion of the usual chess scale where human players loom over inch-tall figures. The scale flip is the narrative. The pieces are the protagonists; the players are small, frightened, and tactical. The pavilion borrows this: the four pedestal pieces are scaled up so visitors walk beneath them as supplicants. Architecture and scale together decide who the camera is pointing at, and therefore whose hierarchy we're inside.

Source: shot-analysis write-ups of the 2001 film's chess sequence; comparative note from Film Art: An Introduction (Bordwell & Thompson).

What changed because of this

Three decisions in the final pavilion are directly traceable to these insights. The refusal to assign a single label to each piece (King is both Dumbledore and figurehead-CEO and Minister) came out of insight five. Putting the pieces on pedestals inside a deliberately tall chamber came out of four. The choice to keep Ron's sacrifice as the pivot beat of the chess sequence came out of three — without the sacrifice the hierarchy has no mechanism, and the pavilion is just a monument to decoration.

The pavilion is not trying to solve hierarchy. It is trying to make it visible.
